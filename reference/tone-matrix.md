# Neobank Tone Matrix

Five tone archetypes for B2C neobanks. Pick one based on the bank's ICP and positioning. Match the closest archetype, then write inside that lane. Do not blend two archetypes in the same surface area, that is how brands end up sounding generic.

Each archetype has 5 patterns to use, 5 to avoid, and 1 reference brand to study.

## 1. Mass consumer

**Examples:** Chime, Varo, Current, Dave.
**Audience:** Paycheck-to-paycheck Americans, near-prime credit, underbanked or recently banked, makes financial decisions on Friday afternoons.
**Voice:** Warm, paycheck-solidarity, plain language, "your money," "we're on your side."

### Use these patterns
1. "Your money" framing. Money belongs to the user, not the bank. Chime's "Get paid 2 days early. Why should you wait? It's your money." is the canonical example.
2. Time-of-paycheck framing. Talk about Friday, payday, the gap before the next deposit hits, the specific moment money matters.
3. Solidarity language. "We" includes the user. "We get it." "We've been there." "We built this because the old way wasn't working for people like us."
4. Plain numbers. "Get paid up to 2 days early." "No overdraft fees." "$0 monthly fees." Round, concrete, no asterisks in the headline.
5. Anti-bank framing. Position against the legacy banks, fee structures, and predatory products. Frame the user as smart for choosing better.

### Avoid these patterns
1. Aspirational wealth language. No "build your empire," no "elite," no "exclusive."
2. Investment-account voice. No "grow your portfolio," no "compound your wealth."
3. Premium-tier ladders pushed in core copy. Save tier upsells for the upgrade surface, not the homepage.
4. Jargon. No "interchange," no "APY" without context, no "regulatory."
5. Cold institutional voice. No "members," no "clients," no "valued customer."

**Reference brand:** Chime. Study their homepage, their app store screenshots, and their "no overdraft fees" campaign.

## 2. Premium consumer

**Examples:** Revolut (especially Metal and Ultra tiers), SoFi premium tiers.
**Audience:** Mid-to-high income, 25-45, urban, identity tied to ambition and control over their financial life.
**Voice:** Aspirational, control narrative, premium-tier ladder.

### Use these patterns
1. Control language. "Manage every account from one place." "Move money in seconds." "Run your money the way you run your life."
2. Tier-ladder framing. Free, Plus, Premium, Metal, Ultra. Make the ladder visible. Each tier has a clear, named upgrade reason.
3. Global and multi-currency framing. "Spend in 150 currencies." "No foreign transaction fees." "One card, every country."
4. Design-led copy. Short, confident, sentence-fragment. Every line earns its place. Match a clean visual system.
5. Outcome framing for the ambitious user. "Build wealth." "Hit your goals." "Compound your money." Allowed here because the audience opts in.

Revolut spent $591M on marketing in 2024 chasing this audience. The tone matters at scale.

### Avoid these patterns
1. Paycheck-solidarity language. The audience does not see themselves as paycheck-to-paycheck.
2. Discount or fee-fight framing. Premium audiences do not lead with "save $5 a month."
3. Anti-bank populism. The legacy bank is not the enemy here, friction and slowness are.
4. Casual or meme voice. Premium audiences read it as cheap.
5. Cluttered hero copy. One value, one line. If two lines, both are short.

**Reference brand:** Revolut. Study their tier upgrade flows, the Metal and Ultra positioning pages, and their out-of-home campaigns.

## 3. Cultural

**Examples:** Cash App.
**Audience:** Subculture-embedded, hip-hop and creator economy adjacent, money-as-gesture, peer-to-peer first.
**Voice:** Irreverent, subculture-embedded, money-as-gesture.

### Use these patterns
1. Subculture-native phrases. "Cash App me" became cultural shorthand because Cash App earned it through years of Travis Scott, Megan Thee Stallion, and Kendrick Lamar mentions.
2. Money as gesture, not utility. Tipping, gifting, splitting, sending. The transaction is a moment, not a chore.
3. Visual identity over verbose copy. Big numbers, big icons, short lines.
4. Creator and artist partnerships referenced in the marketing surface. Make the cultural alliance visible.
5. Self-aware tone. Acknowledge being part of culture, not selling at it.

### Avoid these patterns
1. Corporate bank language. No "financial wellness," no "money management."
2. Premium-tier ladders front and center. Cash App has paid features but does not lead with them.
3. Heavy compliance copy in the hero surface. Move it down the page or behind a footer.
4. Lifestyle stock photography. The audience can smell it.
5. Performative diversity language. The cultural fit must be earned in the partnerships, not stated in the copy.

**Reference brand:** Cash App. Study their feature launches, their music video integrations, and the way "Cash App me" entered cultural shorthand.

## 4. Community-transparent

**Examples:** Monzo.
**Audience:** Engaged, community-leaning, slightly older skew (28-45), values transparency and cares how a company behaves.
**Voice:** Radical transparency, learning in public, public unit economics.

### Use these patterns
1. Transparency moves. Publish the financials, publish the roadmap, publish the failures. Monzo built its early audience this way.
2. Community-first language. "Our community," "we built this with you," "your feedback shaped this."
3. Public learning. Blog posts that explain how the bank works, what went wrong, what is being changed.
4. Plain, calm voice. Not frantic, not aspirational, not performative. Confident and steady.
5. Campaign anchors that lean into emotion without overselling. Monzo's "Money Never Felt Like Monzo" 2024 campaign is the canonical example, money as a felt experience, not a product feature.

### Avoid these patterns
1. Hype language. The audience reacts against it.
2. Aggressive growth copy. Community-leaning audiences punish brands that look like they are chasing growth at the cost of the community.
3. Manufactured exclusivity. Waitlist tactics work once, then erode trust.
4. Vague product claims. The audience reads the fine print and notices when claims are loose.
5. Performative populism. Do not claim to be the people's bank without evidence.

**Reference brand:** Monzo. Study their public blog posts, their community forum interactions, and the "Money Never Felt Like Monzo" 2024 campaign.

## 5. Niche affinity

**Examples:** Greenwood (Black community), Step (teen banking with parental oversight), First Boulevard (Black community), Daylight (LGBTQ+, now closed but the playbook remains).
**Audience:** Identity-coded community where the bank's existence is itself the value proposition.
**Voice:** Identity-recognition first, community-coded, inclusion language.

### Use these patterns
1. Identity-recognition opener. The first thing a new visitor sees should signal "this is for you, by people like you."
2. Community-coded mechanics. Greenwood's cash-back-at-Black-businesses mechanic is the textbook example. The product mechanic itself reinforces the identity claim.
3. Founders and team visibility. Niche-affinity audiences want to see who built it.
4. Inclusion language that is specific and earned. Not generic "diverse team," but "founded by [specific people] for [specific community]."
5. Community-investment narrative. Where the deposits go, who benefits, what circulates back. Niche-affinity audiences expect the bank to participate in the community, not just serve it.

### Avoid these patterns
1. Generic challenger-bank copy. The whole point is that this is not a generic neobank.
2. Tokenizing language. Do not name the community in every sentence, that reads as performative.
3. Performative mission statements without product mechanics behind them. The audience checks.
4. Ignoring the dual-product reality. Niche-affinity neobanks still need to earn interchange and route direct deposits, the identity layer sits on top of the same unit economics.
5. Stock imagery that is not community-specific. The audience can spot a generic stock model in one second.

**Reference brand:** Greenwood. Study their cash-back-at-Black-businesses mechanic, their founder visibility, and the way their identity claim is encoded into the product itself, not just the copy.

## How to use this matrix

1. Match the bank's ICP to the closest archetype.
2. Write inside that archetype's "use these patterns" list.
3. Run a final pass against the "avoid these patterns" list before sending to compliance.
4. Spend 30 minutes studying the reference brand before any major launch. Look at their actual landing pages, app store listings, and most-recent ad creative.

If a bank truly does not fit any archetype, the bank's positioning is probably the problem, not the matrix. Run the `comms-bedrock` or `dunford-positioning` skill before continuing.
